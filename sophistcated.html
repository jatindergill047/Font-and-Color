Every 9/11 I reflect. Today I was thinking about the collective mind of the nation. It’s been seventeen years since the incident, and the psychosis of modern life in America is an attestation of the hidden pain and trauma ingrained in the contemporary American animus. Everyday there is a mass shooting, suicide rates are at an all time high, US citizens are over-medicating pain and depression, and prospects just seem dimmer and dimmer as retribution politics play out on the world stage. As of today, the United States uses a few coping mechanisms to work through the pain of the incident that occurred on September 11, 2001. These coping mechanisms include memes, ritualistic (yet void) “thoughts and prayers” types of televised memorials, and empty symbolic propaganda. Each one serves a purpose for individuals as well as the collective.

Memes are a popular form of communication. Memes are pictures with captions, used to quickly display wit and references. In context to September 11th, memes serve as a coping mechanism, as humor masks pain. Currently 9/11 is seen as a day for “meme farmers” to share their works across all of the social networks. The memes are crass, ironic, nihilistic, self-referential, meta, political, and always integrating the newest ideas. These pixaled jokes are the memetic representation of the trauma that surrounds this incident. Memes, while expressing irony and nihilism also express a kind of hopelessness surrounding 9/11. As funny as the memes are, there is always a sadness that shadows the humor. This is the trauma. Next, the void yet reflective pomp and circumstance of the yearly ritual remembrance of those who were lost. These have been reduced to televised symbol worship and “thoughts and prayers” type of reflexive, vacant memorials. A flag waves, the soldier plays taps at 10:28 am, a moment of silence, and the world moves on. But America doesn’t. These memorials, while helpful for the families affected by the tragedy, are not helpful for the collective mental state of the US. These memorial ceremonies are typically accompanied by archival (yet censored and edited) live footage of the event. This rips open old wounds, and pours salt on that unhealed emotional trauma. One good aspect of these is that those watching typically reminisce with people they are around. Same on the social networks. Someone always asks “Where were you?” or, “What memory do you have of the day?”. Most share some detail of seeing the towers fall, a feeling, a flash. Over time, memories change, details blur. Yet the trauma remains.

Unlike people born before 2001, people born after the date don’t have the memories to recall what happened on that day and shortly thereafter. The young people are left with a paragraph or a blurb in scantly detailed history books that have been as sanitized as the footage of the jumpers from both buildings. People argue its better that the young people don’t know. I disagree, because when this incident occurred, I was young. I had just graduated high school. No one told me that history would be sanitized to fit the current popular narrative. Because of my lack of knowledge of how governments take advantage of events like 9/11, and the lack of knowledge of so many young people, Americans rights got systematically stripped in the name of security. The only way this can be told, because the schoolbooks are obviously inadequate, is to teach them. I was reading an article today about the events that conspired. This particular article was a difficult read, as it detailed who the jumpers were and the feelings and thoughts of the families as well as postulation as to what would compel so many people to leap to their fates, to the pavement below. This article, “The Falling Man”, by Tom Junod, articulated how the US has self censored the pictures and videos of the jumpers, relegating the footage to the darkest corners of the internet. So obscured are the images and videos that these days the unedited footage can only be found on such backwater torture porn sites such as Rotten.com, and the NY coroners office archives.
